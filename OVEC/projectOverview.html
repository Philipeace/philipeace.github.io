Project Overview: Orbital Visualizer for Electron Configurations (OVEC)

Welcome to the OVEC (Orbital Visualizer for Electron Configurations) project. This platform is designed with an atomic physical styling, highlighting quantum-mechanical features of electrons in atoms. The overall aesthetic aims to provide a gentle, educational vibe while still showcasing the complexity of wavefunctions.

Purpose and Goals

The main goal of OVEC is to provide an interactive way to explore electron orbitals for various elements (up to Z=118). By integrating a user-friendly periodic table, wavefunction sampling, and real-time 3D rendering, we aim to educate and delight students, researchers, and science enthusiasts in the field of atomic physics.

Key Features

  • Periodic Table Integration: Quickly pick elements and auto-set protons/neutrons (with potential isotope support).
  • Configurable Electron Count: Explore ions or excited states by altering electron counts beyond neutrality.
  • Atomic Physical Style: Real spherical harmonics, simplified Slater-based Zeff, radial wavefunction sampling.
  • 3D Visualization: Large point clouds using Three.js, with luminous additive blending for striking orbital shapes.
  • Radial Plot Animations: Inline small previews, plus bigger pop-up canvases for detailed wavefunction inspection.
  • Performance Tweaks: Adjust point counts, sampling strategies, and shading to balance realism vs. speed.

Advanced Use Cases & Limitations

OVEC uses a hydrogenic model for multi-electron atoms, applying Slater’s rules for approximate shielding. Thus, real multi-electron interactions are not fully captured. For conceptual demos and teaching, the approach works well. For advanced research or high-accuracy calculations, methods like Hartree–Fock or DFT are necessary.

Note: For high-Z elements (e.g., Z=118), acceptance rates may be low with standard rejection sampling. Future plans include more sophisticated sampling algorithms or GPU acceleration for large point sets.

Target Audience

  • Students & Educators: High school or undergraduate physics/chemistry for a visual demonstration of orbitals.
  • Hobbyists: Anyone curious about quantum mechanical structures in atoms.
  • Classroom Demos: Great for lectures, workshops, or outreach events on fundamental atomic theory.

Future Directions

  • Isotope Support: Deeper modeling of nuclear properties (beyond just changing N) and potential isotope visual comparisons.
  • Web Worker & GPU Approaches: Offload large sampling tasks from the main thread for smoother UI experiences.
  • Customizable Color Maps: Let users choose color ramps to emphasize node structures or radial distances more clearly.
